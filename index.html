Should I Buy the SpaceX IPO?

A friend asked me a simple question. A few weeks of digging later, I’m not sure the question was simple at all — and what I found is worth sharing.

Satya Thangavel June 2026 ~9 min read

A buddy of mine — the kind of guy who has built real wealth in real estate and knows exactly what he doesn’t know about tech — texted me a days ago. “Hey, can a regular person get in on the SpaceX, Anthropic, and ChatGPT IPOs? Worth it?” Reasonable question. Three of the most famous companies on earth, all going public at roughly the same moment. I figured I’d spend twenty minutes, send him a clean answer, move on.

I did not move on. The deeper I went, the more I realized the interesting story isn’t whether these companies are good. It’s the plumbing underneath the listings — a set of quiet rule changes and accounting mechanics that, once you see them, you can’t unsee. So instead of a text back, he got an essay. Here’s the cleaned-up version, because I think more people should understand what’s actually happening before the opening bell rings.

Let me start with the scale, because the numbers alone are hard to hold in your head.

01 — The setupThe three biggest IPOs in history, stacked on top of each other

SpaceX is going public on the Nasdaq under the ticker SPCX, targeting a valuation of about $1.77 trillion at $135 a share, raising roughly $75 billion. If it prices where it’s aiming, it’s the largest IPO ever recorded — it would shatter the record Saudi Aramco set in 2019. The roadshow is already underway and shares are expected to start trading in mid-June.

Right behind it: Anthropic, the company behind the Claude AI models, confidentially filed for its own IPO on June 1, fresh off a funding round that valued it near $965 billion. And OpenAI, the maker of ChatGPT, has been laying the groundwork for a listing of its own that could approach $1 trillion. Three companies. Close to $4 trillion in combined value. Hitting the public market within a few months of each other.

CNBC called it a potential test of whether 2026 becomes “the most consequential IPO cycle since the dot-com era.” That framing stuck with me, because the dot-com comparison is exactly where this gets uncomfortable.

02 — The part nobody mentionsThe rulebook was quietly rewritten first

Here’s where my twenty minutes turned into a few days. To understand why this matters to your money — not just to billionaires — you have to understand how index funds work. Stick with me, this is the key.

When you own an S&P 500 or Nasdaq-100 index fund (and if you have a 401(k), you almost certainly do), you’re not picking stocks. The fund mechanically buys whatever is in the index funds like SPY, QQQ, in proportion to its size. So getting added to a major index isn’t a popularity contest — it’s a money cannon. The moment a company joins, hundreds of billions of dollars in passive funds are forced to buy it, whether the fund manager thinks it’s a good deal or not.

Now here’s the thing. Two rules used to stand between a company like SpaceX and that money cannon. And in March 2026, the Nasdaq changed both of them, effective May 1 — weeks before these listings.

What changed — Nasdaq-100 “Fast Entry” rule
3 mo → 15 days
The waiting period before a new company can join the index dropped from roughly three months (in practice, often up to a year) to about 15 trading days.
Float min: gone
The old rule required at least 10% of shares to be publicly available. SpaceX plans to float only ~4–5%. Under the old rule, it wouldn’t have qualified. The minimum was eliminated.
3× weight
Low-float companies now get their index weight inflated up to three times their actual float — so a 5% float can be treated like 15%, forcing funds to buy more than the tradable supply would justify.

I want to be fair here: Nasdaq’s stated reasoning is defensible. Genuinely huge companies were being structurally locked out of an index meant to represent them, and the consultation had broad support. You can read the methodology change straight from the source. But the timing is the timing. A trading-research firm put it plainly when it noted that critics calling the changes a “grift” have a real point — and it’s not just Nasdaq. S&P and FTSE Russell are loosening their own rules in parallel.

A handful of insiders got in early at low prices. To exit at these valuations, they need an enormous buyer. The rule changes quietly made your retirement account that buyer.

That’s the uncomfortable mechanic. Not a conspiracy with a smoke-filled room — just incentives lining up a little too neatly.

03 — Is the rocket company actually worth it?Three businesses wearing one price tag

So I dug into SpaceX’s actual financials, which it disclosed publicly for the first time in its filing. Most people think “SpaceX” means rockets and Mars. It’s really three very different businesses bolted together, and the numbers tell a split story.

SpaceX, by segment (2025)
Starlink
The engine. ~$11.4B revenue, ~$4.4B operating profit, ~63% margins, 10.3M subscribers across 164 countries. A genuinely great, scaled business.
Rockets
Launch and government contracts. Real, lower-margin, the part everyone pictures — but a minority of the revenue.
xAI
Elon’s AI company, merged in this year. Roughly $3.2B revenue against a ~$6.4B operating loss. It burns more than a billion dollars a month.

Put it together and the picture flips. SpaceX was profitable on its own in 2024 — about $791 million in the black. After absorbing xAI, it posted a ~$4.94 billion net loss on $18.7 billion in revenue in 2025. Starlink makes money. Rockets make money. xAI lights it on fire. You’re being asked to buy all of that bundled at the highest IPO valuation in human history.

And here’s a detail the headlines skip: Starlink’s revenue-per-user is actually falling — from around $99 a month to about $66 — as it expands into cheaper international markets. The growth is real, but it’s volume papering over softening unit economics. Morningstar, for what it’s worth, values the whole company at about $780 billion — less than half the IPO target.

04 — The mechanic that genuinely surprised meThe money is moving in a circle

This is the part I’d want my friend to understand more than anything else, because it’s subtle and it’s real. The AI boom you’re reading about in the headlines — record profits, trillion-dollar valuations — is partly built on companies paying each other in a loop.

A cloud giant invests billions in an AI lab →
the lab spends that money renting the giant’s cloud →
the giant books it as revenue, and marks up its stake as profit →
uses those gains to justify spending even more → repeat.

These aren’t small deals. OpenAI expanded a $38 billion cloud agreement with Amazon by another $100 billion. It committed a quarter-trillion dollars to Microsoft’s Azure. Anthropic committed $30 billion to Azure while Microsoft and Nvidia put $15 billion into Anthropic. Bloomberg mapped the whole web of it, and together OpenAI and Anthropic now account for more than half of the roughly $2 trillion in future cloud commitments held by the big four cloud providers. To supporters, this is a “virtuous circle” locking in scarce computing power. To skeptics, it’s a hall of mirrors.

And then there’s the accounting piece, which is the real eye-opener. Because these cloud giants own equity stakes in the AI labs, every time a lab raises money at a higher valuation, the giant gets to record that paper gain as profit. In one recent quarter, a large share of both Alphabet’s and Amazon’s reported profit came from unrealized markups on their AI stakes — gains where no cash actually changed hands. Amazon’s free cash flow in that same window reportedly collapsed by 95% as it poured tens of billions into data centers.

Most bubbles show up in the price. This one might be hiding in the earnings.

That’s the cleverest distinction I came across, and it’s worth slowing down on. In a normal bubble, prices detach from earnings and the price-to-earnings ratio screams at you. Here, the P/E ratios look reasonable — because the earnings themselves are being propped up by paper gains and circular revenue. An inflated bottom line makes everything look healthier than it is. We’ve seen that movie before, with bank and homebuilder earnings that looked pristine right up until 2008.

It’s not theoretical math, either. When the Financial Times modeled the returns these companies are likely to earn on their massive AI spending — under the most generous, zero-cost assumptions possible — most of them came out negative. Microsoft, Google, Meta, and Oracle all underwater; only Amazon barely positive. That’s the best case.

05 — The backdropThis is all happening on shaky ground

If this were a calm year, maybe none of it would matter much. It is not a calm year. While I was researching, the macro picture kept getting heavier:

The Strait of Hormuz has been effectively closed for about three months following the conflict with Iran — choking off roughly a fifth of the world’s oil supply. Inflation has reignited (CPI back up near 3.8%, gasoline up sharply), and analysts have floated oil running toward $150 a barrel if it drags on. Meanwhile, Kevin Warsh was just sworn in as the new Fed chair, walking into a near-impossible bind: cut rates into an inflation spike and risk making it worse, or hold and raise borrowing costs right as a trillion dollars of debt-funded AI spending needs cheap money. Bond markets are already nervous.

None of these are predictions. They’re just the conditions these record-breaking listings are launching into.

06 — The thing my friend didn’t realizeYou might already own this bet

Here’s what stopped me cold, and what I think most people miss. My friend asked how to buy in. But if he owns a standard index fund, he’s already all-in.

AI-related stocks now make up somewhere between 45% and 50% of the entire S&P 500 by value. J.P. Morgan flagged that the top 20 companies alone are about 51% of the index — a concentration higher than the dot-com peak. The market keeps hitting record highs, but strip out the AI names and the other ~480 companies are basically flat. The index is being carried by a handful of giants, and within weeks those giants will be joined by the biggest IPOs in history.

So when people say “just buy the index, it’s diversified” — that’s less true than it used to be. Roughly half of that “diversified” fund is now a single thematic bet on artificial intelligence. That’s not necessarily wrong. But you should at least know it’s the bet you’re making.

07 — So what did I tell him?Technology winning and investors winning are two different things

Here’s the part I keep coming back to, because history is weirdly clear on it. In the late 1800s, the railroads transformed America. The technology absolutely won — the rails are still here. But most of the investors who funded the boom got wiped out; the fortunes were made later by people who bought the assets out of bankruptcy. Same story with the fiber-optic cable boom of the 1990s: the companies that laid the cable went bust in the dot-com crash, and a decade later that very fiber became the backbone of the internet you’re reading this on.

I think AI is real. I build with these tools every day; they’re changing how I work. Starlink is one of the most impressive things built this decade. But “the technology will change the world” and “the people buying at a $1.77 trillion valuation will be rewarded” are two completely separate claims — and only the first one is obviously true.

So here’s what I actually told my friend, and it wasn’t a yes or a no. First: know what you already own. Before chasing a new IPO, understand that your existing index funds are about to hold these companies automatically, at whatever price the market sets on day one. Second: the “guaranteed” buying is real but it’s bounded — fast-entry only applies to the very largest names, and the weighting is capped. And third: nobody actually knows how this ends. Maybe the listings go great and the boom justifies itself. Maybe it’s 2000 in slow motion. The job isn’t to be right about which — it’s to size your bets so you survive being wrong.

That’s a less exciting answer than “yes, get rich” or “no, it’s all a scam.” But it’s the honest one. And honestly, the rabbit hole was worth it — I understand the machine a lot better now than I did when that text came in.

One caveat, clearly stated: I’m a technologist, not a financial advisor, and none of this is investment advice. It’s me sharing the trail I followed and the sources I used so you can check the facts yourself and reach your own conclusions. Every figure here was accurate as of writing; these deals are moving fast, so verify before you act on anything.

If you found this useful, the most valuable thing you can do is the same thing I did — follow the links, read the primary sources, and think for yourself. That’s the whole point.
